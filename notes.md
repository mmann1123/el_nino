
# El Salvador maize monitoring notes

So if I had to name the tight operational core for El Salvador maize next season, it is: CHIRPS v3 + CHIRPS3-GEFS for forward-looking rainfall, SMAP L4 root-zone for the soil-water state going into and through silking, and SSEBop v6 ETa anomaly to confirm crop stress — all aggregated to the eastern Dry Corridor municipios and refreshed on roughly a 5-day cadence through the May–August primera window — with HLS/VIIRS as lagging vegetation confirmation and FEWS NET EWX / JRC ASAP as the integrated wrapper. A concrete trigger worth pre-registering: SPI-3 below −1 and SMAP L4 root-zone anomaly below −1σ during the mid-July to mid-August silking window, confirmed by an SSEBop ETa negative anomaly within the following dekad.

The strongest leading indicator is the pairing of CHIRPS v3 daily/pentad with the CHIRPS3-GEFS 15-day forecast. CHIRPS at 0.05° is your anomaly and seasonal-cycle anchor (compute SPI-1, SPI-3, SPI-6 against a 1981–2020 baseline), and the GEFS forecast extension is the only piece in the entire stack that buys you genuine lead time — a 5/10/15-day rainfall outlook lets you see a deepening or extending canícula before it desiccates the root zone. One operational note that matters for next season: the CHIRPS2-GEFS stream stops updating July 1, 2026, so you must be on CHIRPS3-GEFS before the canícula. Pair this with IMERG-Late (0.1°, ~14-hour latency) for daily event verification, since CHIRPS daily is weak at the event scale in this region and IMERG better preserves the orographic structure across El Salvador's volcanic chain and eastern rain-shadow zones.

CHIRPS3 includes a new daily 'sat' product that uses NASA IMERG-Late V07 to partition pentadal CHIRPS-v3 totals into daily amounts. CHIRPS-GEFSv12 ("CHIRPS3-GEFS") provides bias-corrected, CHIRPS-compatible 16-day forecasts at 0.05°, updated daily for 5/10/15-day windows and on pentadal/dekadal schedules. The 16-day GEFS forecasts are bias-corrected via rank-based quantile matching against the CHIRPS historical distribution, retaining GEFS's forecast skill while aligning to CHIRPS climatology and variance. Critically, operational updates of the CHIRPS2-GEFS stream will end July 1, 2026; users must migrate to CHIRPS3-GEFS before then. Documented GEFS positive bias of >40 mm in central Mexico, Central America, and northern South America is exactly what the bias correction removes. Ucsb + 2

The single best near-real-time soil signal for maize is SMAP L4 root-zone soil moisture (SPL4SMGP, 9 km, ~2.5–7 day latency). Surface soil moisture (SMAP L3) is less useful here — what kills maize yield at silking is depletion of the 0–100 cm root-zone store, which is exactly what L4 models and assimilates, and it keeps producing through SMAP instrument outages because it is model-backed. At 9 km it is too coarse for individual milpa plots but well-matched to municipio-level anomaly tracking (eastern Dry Corridor departments — Morazán, San Miguel, La Unión, Usulután — are your priority). If you want finer texture, add the Copernicus SCATSAR-SWI 1 km (daily, sub-24-hour latency) as a higher-resolution flash-drought layer.

Earth Engine: NASA/SMAP/SPL3SMP_E/006 (data from 4 December 2023–present; v005 covers 2015–3 December 2023) and NASA/SMAP/SPL4SMGP/008. L4 provides 0–5 cm surface and 0–100 cm root-zone soil moisture — the latter essential because the canícula desiccates root-zone storage that the L-band radiometer alone cannot see.


The best confirming crop-water-stress signal is SSEBop v6 actual-ET anomaly (1 km, dekadal, ~10–20 day latency). This is the closest open product to an actual evaporative-stress measurement on the crop, and the ETa anomaly is purpose-built for drought monitoring — USGS cautions to use the anomaly rather than absolute magnitudes in a region without local bias correction, which is fine for your purpose. It lags the rainfall and soil-moisture signals by a dekad or so, so treat it as confirmation that stress is translating into reduced transpiration, not as an early warning.

SPI is the Standardized Precipitation Index — the most widely used meteorological drought index, developed by McKee, Doesken, and Kleist in 1993.SPI is the Standardized Precipitation Index — the most widely used meteorological drought index, developed by McKee, Doesken, and Kleist in 1993.
The core idea is simple: it tells you how unusual the precipitation over some recent period has been, compared to the long-term history for that same location and same time of year, expressed in standard-deviation units. Mechanically, you take the accumulated precipitation over a chosen window (say the last 3 months), fit a probability distribution to the full historical record of that same window — precipitation is right-skewed and has lots of zeros, so a gamma distribution is the standard choice rather than a normal one — and then transform that fitted distribution onto a standard normal scale. The result is a z-score-like number centered on zero.
