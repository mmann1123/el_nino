# El Niño Risk to Staple Food Crops in El Salvador and Haiti: Biophysical Lessons from 1997-98, 2015-16, and 2023-24 Applied to the 2026-27 Event

## TL;DR

- **The 2026-27 event is near-certain but its peak intensity is not yet fixed.** The 14 May 2026 NOAA CPC discussion puts the probability of El Niño at 82% for May-July 2026 and 96% for December 2026-February 2027; the IRI/CCSR plume holds El Niño probabilities in a "remarkably high and narrow 97–98% range" through JFM 2027. CPC adds, however, that "no strength categorization exceeding a 37% chance," and ECMWF's C3S April 2026 multi-system ensemble for the September peak ranges from +0.2°C to +3.3°C in Niño-3.4, with a 13-model, 637-run multi-model median near +2.2°C (Carbon Brief). The framing of an "acute" or "super" 2026-27 El Niño is plausible but not established — expect strong, not necessarily extreme.
- **The historical analogs point to a tight biophysical signature in both countries.** A strengthened Pacific Walker cell and Caribbean Low-Level Jet (CLLJ) suppress July-October convection over the Pacific slope of Central America and the Greater Antilles, elongating the canícula by 20+ days, driving SPEI into moderate-to-severe drought, and clipping rainfed maize and bean yields in the primera/printemps and postrera/automne seasons by 30-80% in worst-affected smallholder zones. In Haiti, irrigated Artibonite rice was largely buffered in 2015-16 (USDA FAS yield held at 2.5 MT/ha rough basis), while rainfed maize fell ~39% and beans ~44% nationally relative to 2014 (FEWS NET, Feb 2016). In El Salvador's eastern Dry Corridor, smallholder milpa systems lost 60% of primera maize and 80% of beans in 2015 (FAO/FEWS NET).
- **The most actionable biophysical priorities for 2026-27 are threefold.** First, prepare for a delayed primera/printemps start, an extended canícula, and a high-probability poor postrera/automne in both countries. Second, anticipate boreal-spring 2027 rainfall to be near-normal-to-wetter in Haiti and the Caribbean (the "El Niño +1 year" wet ERS signal documented by Chen & Taylor 2002 in *International Journal of Climatology*), offering a possible rebound window for printemps 2027. Third, recognise that climate-change-elevated baseline temperatures (MARN reports a ~0.35°C/decade rise in El Salvador, implying ~+1.4°C over the past four decades) will amplify evaporative demand beyond 1997-98 analog yields, biasing crop water stress upward even at the same precipitation deficit.

## Key Findings

**ENSO mechanism over the region.** El Niño's footprint on Central American and Caribbean agriculture is mediated by three coupled processes operating chiefly in boreal summer-fall. First, a warmer eastern equatorial Pacific shifts and weakens the Walker circulation, producing anomalous subsidence over the Caribbean Sea and intermountain Central America — a mechanism that emerges as the dominant single driver in recent CMIP6 decomposition by Vichot-Llano et al. and Hassler et al. (*Climate Dynamics* 2023). Second, the Caribbean Low-Level Jet — an easterly trade-wind jet maximized in July and February — intensifies during El Niño summers, driving moisture flux divergence over the western Caribbean and reducing convective rainfall on the Pacific slope of Central America (Whyte et al. 2008; Martínez et al. 2020, *Climate Dynamics*). Third, the upper-tropospheric westerly anomalies that suppress Atlantic tropical cyclogenesis (Gray 1984) also reduce the contribution of TC-related precipitation to August-November totals — particularly relevant to Haiti, where Moron et al. (2015, *Climate Dynamics* 45:915-932) show October-November rainfall variability is governed by tropical-depression track recurrence northeast of Hispaniola. FEWS NET synthesises the seasonal signal cleanly: across Central America and the Caribbean, "the likelihood of drier than normal conditions increase[s] during June-October" during El Niño events, based on the 22 events since 1950.

**El Salvador: the Dry Corridor amplifier.** El Salvador sits entirely within the Central American Dry Corridor (Corredor Seco Centroamericano), which Quesada-Hernández et al. (2020) and the FAO (2012) characterise as the strip of Pacific-slope Central America where mean annual rainfall already decreases by roughly 30-40% in El Niño years. The CGIAR Alliance report *El fenómeno de El Niño en Guatemala* (2024) quantifies this for the agricultural season May-October: "El Niño years exhibit a widespread reduction of up to 20% in precipitation and an average increase of 1°C in temperature… potential evapotranspiration experiences a 7% increase compared to the historical average. The frequency of drought events, assessed through the Standardized Precipitation Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI), indicates that over 60% of El Niño events between 1980 and 2021 involved moderate droughts during the agricultural season, especially in the Dry Corridor." Under El Niño, FEWS NET documents the canícula's duration extending from a climatological 10-15 days in July to 25-40+ days, coinciding precisely with the silking and grain-filling stages of primera maize — the V8-through-silking phenology where maize yield-water-stress curves are steepest. For 2015 specifically, FEWS NET documented cumulative July 26-August 24 rainfall in much of El Salvador at 25-50% of average, with localised totals of 5-25% of average; primera-season drought ran 20 consecutive days (20 July-8 August) in the eastern zone.

**El Salvador, historical losses by event.** The 2015-16 El Niño produced the most quantitatively documented event. FEWS NET (August 2015) reported that primera rainfall anomalies caused total loss of 56,700 ha of maize plus partial damage to 29,100 ha, and total loss of 2,400 ha of beans plus partial damage to 473 ha — together representing ~97% of the primera cropped area for these staples. National FAO/GIEWS figures put 2015 maize destruction at 60% and beans at 80%, with US$28 million in lost inputs alone (FAO, September 2015); cumulative 2014-15 drought losses exceeded US$140 million (Adaptation Fund / DIGESTYC). Total 2015 rainy-season cumulative precipitation reached only 63% of the climatological normal — the lowest on record at the time. The 2023-24 event was milder; FEWS NET (October 2023) projected a one-month delay to the postrera harvest with subsistence yield reductions of at least 25%, alongside slight reductions in commercial production, and the February 2024 update confirmed that this projection had materialised. The 1997-98 event primarily damaged second-season maize in El Salvador; FAO reported that "considerably below-average" 1997/98 second-season maize was harvested, though beans were less affected because of normal late-1997 rains. Damage was overshadowed politically by Hurricane Mitch in October-November 1998 (a transition to La Niña), which destroyed roughly US$158 million of Salvadoran agriculture (IDB).

**Smallholder vs. commercial asymmetry in El Salvador.** El Salvador's basic-grains sector is dominated by smallholders, with the average milpa plot ~0.7 ha producing 1,427 kg/ha of maize against a national mean of 2,575 kg/ha (DIGESTYC). Smallholder yields collapse more catastrophically during El Niño because they are rainfed, lack irrigation buffer, and depend on saved seed that can be exhausted by a single failed primera — exactly the cascade observed in 2015 when primera bean failure left insufficient seed for the postrera planting (UN News 2018). Commercial irrigated maize, concentrated in larger holdings on the coastal plain, typically experiences yield drops in the 10-25% range rather than total loss. Eitzinger et al. (2017) using DSSAT modeling project that under continued warming El Salvador will see ~7% bean yield reductions and >15% maize reductions under poor-soil scenarios by 2025 — and these are baseline mean-climate projections, with El Niño anomalies superimposed on top.

**Haiti: a teleconnection with a sting and a recovery.** Giannini, Kushnir & Cane (2000, *Journal of Climate* 13:297-311) established the canonical Caribbean ENSO signal: warm eastern Pacific SST is associated with negative rainfall anomalies in the Caribbean during July-August and September-October of the El Niño year; subsequently, in November-December and January-February, "a positive tongue of warm waters extends from the eastern Pacific into the Caribbean-western Atlantic basin," producing anomalous tropical-North-Atlantic warming with about a season's delay relative to the Pacific El Niño. Chen & Taylor (2002, *International Journal of Climatology*) extended this to demonstrate "a wet early rainfall season of the year of ENSO decline (the El Niño + 1 year)." Moron et al. (2015) show that for Haiti specifically, lower-than-average rainfall is statistically tied to positive SLP anomalies over the tropical North Atlantic and negative SLP anomalies over the eastern Pacific — the El Niño SLP signature — with the August-September-October signal strongest. Jury et al. (2007, *J. Geophys. Res.* 112:D16107) place Hispaniola in their Caribbean subregional cluster C3 (with the Dominican Republic and northwestern Puerto Rico), where the El-Niño-+1-year early-summer ENSO–precipitable-water correlation map shows r > 0.7.

**Haiti, regional heterogeneity.** El Niño impacts in Haiti are not uniform. During the 2015-16 event, FAO's Haiti El Niño Response Plan (September 2016) targeted the Northwest, Southeast, Artibonite (notably the upper Artibonite), Grand'Anse, Nippes, and South departments as worst-hit, identifying 35,000 farming families across 38 of 145 municipalities for relief. FEWS NET's April 2015 outlook noted that "many parts of the North-West, the upper Artibonite, the Central Plateau, and the North-East and a few areas in the Southeast and La Gonâve are still suffering from rainfall deficits. Certain areas like Anse-Rouge in the upper Artibonite, Bombardopolis in the North-West, and the municipality of Anse-à-Galets in La Gonâve have been dry for the past six months." The northern coast (Cap-Haïtien and northeast Haiti), which receives substantial winter rainfall from northeasterly trade-wind interaction with the Massif du Nord, is somewhat decoupled from the August-October Pacific teleconnection; FEWS NET (August 2023) reported the North department had "relatively good or very good" spring production while the southern peninsula and Northwest were already showing El Niño impact. The Central Plateau, in the rain shadow of the Massif du Nord, dries quickly. The Artibonite Valley occupies a special position because rice production there depends primarily on the Péligre dam and the Canal de l'Artibonite irrigation system — about 80,000 hectares of irrigated land in Haiti are dedicated mainly to rice and vegetables (USDA FAS GAIN HA2025-0003).

**Haiti, historical losses by event and crop.**
- *2015-16:* FEWS NET (February 2016) provides the cleanest national figures: maize production fell to 107,600 MT (from 159,600 MT in 2014, "39 percent below the five-year average"); bean production fell to 56,600 MT (from 100,600 MT, "a drop of approximately 44 percent"); other crops (tubers, bananas) declined ~50%. CNSA rapid assessments in worst-hit areas found "72 percent of respondents reported having lost over 80 percent of their crops." The FAO Haiti El Niño Response Plan (September 2016) confirms that "the harvest of the main agricultural season (spring) decreased by over 50 percent," with localised losses up to 70%; FAO's global El Niño synthesis flagged Haiti's 2015 cereal harvest as the lowest in 12 years, with losses as high as 90% in the most affected areas. Importantly, irrigated Artibonite rice was largely buffered: the USDA FAS Haiti rice-yield series shows 2.5 MT/ha (rough basis) sustained through 2015/16, 2016/17, and 2017/18 — meaning irrigation insulated Haiti's largest staple from the worst of the rainfall anomaly, though rainfed sorghum and maize were severely hit.
- *2023-24:* The signal was weaker and confounded by Haiti's compounding political and security crisis. The USDA FAS Commodity Intelligence Report (April 11, 2024) states verbatim: "USDA estimates rice production (milled basis) for marketing year (MY) 2023/24 at 55,000 metric tons (MT), down 8 percent from last year, and down 19 percent from the 5-year average. Corn production for MY 2023/24 is estimated at 305,000 MT, down 2 percent from last year… Sorghum production for MY 2023/24 is estimated at 60,000 MT, down 8 percent from last year, and down 22 percent from the 5-year average. Yield has also declined to 1.82 tons per hectare (rough basis)." USDA explicitly attributes most of the rice decline to "abandonment of some area and the inability to source agricultural inputs due to gang activity," not climate. FAO's DIEM monitoring Round 5 (January 2024) did attribute the January-February 2024 winter-harvest damage to El Niño drought specifically.
- *1997-98:* The 1997-98 super-El Niño produced a milder Haiti agricultural signal in FAO records. FAO/GIEWS in 1998 noted "generally normal weather" benefiting Haiti and the Dominican Republic with "average outputs anticipated"; Hurricane Georges (September 1998), striking during the ENSO transition, did far more damage than the El Niño drought itself. This is a useful corrective to the assumption that all super El Niños hit Haiti hard — the 2015-16 event is the more relevant analog for catastrophic Haiti impacts.

**The hurricane-suppression trade-off in Haiti.** El Niño typically suppresses Atlantic tropical-cyclone activity via increased 200-hPa westerlies and stronger vertical wind shear (Gray 1984; NOAA AOML/Landsea), an effect operating prominently during 2015 and 2023. For Haiti, this is a double-edged sword: TC-related rainfall is a meaningful contributor to August-November totals — Moron et al. (2015) argue that Haitian October-November rainfall variability is "mainly controlled" by intensity, track, and recurrence of tropical depressions traveling northeast of the island — so the same dynamical mechanism that protects Haitian infrastructure from storm damage simultaneously starves the late automne planting season. The literature does not provide a quantitatively defensible Haiti-specific TC-fraction-of-ASO-rainfall estimate (compared with, e.g., the ~75% of annual rainfall during the hurricane season at the Easington site in southern Jamaica per Burn & Palmer 2014); this is a genuine data gap.

**Soil, watershed and antecedent-condition amplifiers.** Both countries enter El Niño droughts with extraordinarily degraded soils that amplify biophysical impacts. El Salvador has roughly 75% of its surface degraded (Pagiola & Dixon 1998, World Bank), with the Andisols on the volcanic chain showing rapid loss of available water capacity under continuous maize-sorghum-bean rotations. The Lempa River basin — Central America's largest — sees river levels drop 20-60% below normal during El Niño years (FAO Dry Corridor SitRep, June 2016), depressing the limited commercial irrigation capacity. Haiti has less than 2% forest cover (FEWS NET), with watershed degradation in the Cul-de-Sac, Artibonite, and Trois-Rivières basins reducing baseflow during droughts and accelerating surface runoff. In Haiti, 90% of agriculture is rainfed (FEWS NET August 2023). Both countries enter the 2026-27 El Niño with degraded antecedent moisture conditions following the 2023-24 El Niño's prolonged drought and an insufficient 2024-25 La Niña recharge: SPEI indices across the Dry Corridor and Haiti remained in the moderate-to-severe drought category through much of 2024-25.

**Temperature and evapotranspiration in a warmed baseline.** El Salvador's MARN reports a national warming trend of approximately 0.35°C per decade, implying roughly +1.4°C since the 1970s; for Haiti, the Ministry of Agriculture and Groupe URD measured a +1°C rise in average atmospheric temperatures between 1973 and 2003. Each El Niño now produces additional warming on top: Daniel Swain (UCLA, May 2026) and Zeke Hausfather (Berkeley Earth) both estimate strong El Niños add 0.2-0.3°C of natural temporary global warming. Crucially for crops, beans (*Phaseolus vulgaris*) are highly sensitive to nighttime temperatures above 18°C, with reduced flowering and pod-set above that threshold (Eitzinger et al. 2017); maize pollen viability collapses above ~35°C ear-level temperatures. The eastern Dry Corridor of El Salvador already experiences these thresholds during canícula in non-El Niño years; under the 2026 event, the WMO Global Seasonal Climate Update (April 2026) projects "nearly global dominance of above-normal land surface temperatures… especially strong over southern North America, Central America, and the Caribbean," suggesting heat stress on flowering primera maize and on senescing postrera beans is the dominant biophysical risk factor.

**The 2026-27 outlook in plain terms.** As of the May 2026 NOAA CPC discussion, El Niño formation is virtually assured (82% MJJ 2026, 96% DJF 2026-27); the IRI multi-model probability is at 98% for May-July 2026 and remains within "a remarkably high and narrow 97–98% range" through JFM 2027. CPC carefully notes, however, that "while confidence in the occurrence of El Niño has increased… there is still substantial uncertainty in the peak strength of El Niño, with no strength categorization exceeding a 37% chance," and that "stronger El Niño events do not ensure strong impacts; they can only make certain impacts more likely." Press coverage characterising this as a "super El Niño" rests on ECMWF dynamical-model outputs; per the ECMWF Science Blog (Tim Stockdale, 10 April 2026), the C3S April 2026 ensemble members for September Niño-3.4 "range from about 1.7 ºC to 3.3 ºC," with the full C3S multi-system range from +0.2°C to +3.3°C, and Carbon Brief's survey of 13 modelling groups and 637 model runs gives a multi-model median of +2.2°C for September 2026 — not the "+3°C with members exceeding +4°C" figure circulating in some popular accounts. The CariCOF Caribbean Climate Outlook Forum (May-July 2026, published April 2026) explicitly states that "Rainfall totals from May to July are likely to be the usual or more in The Bahamas, Cuba and eastern parts of the Guianas, but the usual or less in the ABC Islands, Hispaniola, the US Caribbean Territories and the Lesser Antilles (medium confidence)" — a dry signal for Hispaniola for MJJ 2026. CariCOF's August-October outlook adds that "should a strong El Niño manifest, more intense drought and heat, but less frequent than usual severe weather and tropical cyclone activity may ensue, especially in case intrusions of dusty Saharan air are very frequent."

## Details

### El Salvador biophysics, season by season

The Salvadoran agricultural year operates on a bimodal rainfall regime, with the primera season (May-August) supplying most maize (planted late April-May, harvested August-September) and the postrera (August-November) supplying most beans (planted late August, harvested November-December). The two seasons are punctuated by the canícula or veranillo, climatologically a 10-15 day relative-precipitation minimum in mid-July to early August.

During El Niño, three biophysical shifts compound. First, the onset of primera rains is delayed by 20-40 days, as documented during 2015 by FEWS NET, which compresses the time available for primera-season tasseling and silking before the canícula and forces farmers either to plant late (into the canícula's water deficit) or to gamble on early rains that fail. Second, the canícula itself elongates and intensifies: the FEWS NET 2014 alert (a developing-El Niño year) documented canícula extension "beyond the usual 15 days" with rainfall accumulations 50-75% below average since late June, "the worst dryness in ten years, including the El Niño year of 2009." Third, the postrera season begins with below-average rainfall, with FAO/WFP warning in 2018 that "even if El Niño turns out to be a weak one, it will have a significant impact on the outcome of the second harvest" — a prediction that held in 2018-19 and again in 2023-24.

Soil moisture and shallow groundwater dynamics matter physiologically. The volcanic-derived soils on El Salvador's coastal range have moderate available water capacity (~150-200 mm/m) but lose it rapidly under high vapor-pressure-deficit conditions and bare-soil maize-bean intercropping. The Lempa River basin — Central America's largest, draining most of El Salvador — sees river levels drop 20-60% below normal during El Niño years per FAO's June 2016 Dry Corridor Situation Report, which compromises the small fraction of irrigation-dependent commercial production. Heat stress operates on top of water stress: El Salvador in 2023 recorded daytime temperatures up to 42°C (MARN, Diario de Hoy); if the 2026-27 event produces comparable heatwave layering on top of the rainfall deficit, primera maize at silking in late July could see yield losses exceeding the 2015 baseline.

### Haiti biophysics, season by season

Haiti's agricultural calendar features a printemps (spring) planting from March through May with harvest in July, an été planting in May-July with harvest in August-October, and an automne planting in August-October with harvest in November-February, plus a winter season in irrigated and humid-zone areas. The spring season alone accounts for ~60% of national agricultural production (FEWS NET 2015), making boreal-spring rainfall the single most important determinant of Haitian food security in any given year.

During an El Niño's onset year (e.g., 2015), the boreal-spring rains arrive late or under-perform, the early rainy season (mid-April through June) is irregular, the midsummer drought (which Curtis & Gamble 2007 in *Theoretical and Applied Climatology* show "appears in early-June over Puerto Rico and Hispaniola") is intensified, and the late rainy season (August-October-November) is suppressed by strengthened CLLJ moisture-flux divergence and reduced tropical-cyclone activity. The CMORPH satellite anomalies for 2015 showed cumulative deficits exceeding 200 mm in the worst-affected departments.

In the year following an El Niño peak (the "+1 year"), Caribbean SSTs warm with a 1-2 season lag (the well-known Pacific-to-tropical-North-Atlantic teleconnection of Enfield & Mayer 1997, Giannini et al. 2000, Casselman et al. 2023 in *Weather and Climate Dynamics*), and the early rainy season tends to be wetter than normal. This is why Haiti's spring 2016 rains, even amid lingering El Niño conditions, partially relieved the most acute food-security crisis. For the 2026-27 event, this implies that the spring 2027 rains could be a partial recovery window — provided the event peaks during DJF 2026-27 as forecast and decays through MAM 2027.

Regional heterogeneity within Haiti is substantial. The northern coast (Nord, Nord-Est) and the windward sides of the Massif du Nord receive substantial rainfall from northeasterly trades year-round and are less dependent on the August-October convective rains that El Niño suppresses; FEWS NET (August 2023) reported the North department had "relatively good or very good" spring production while the southern peninsula and Northwest were already showing El Niño impact. The southern peninsula (Sud, Sud-Est, Grand'Anse, Nippes) and the Northwest, which depend on the bimodal Caribbean rainfall and on tropical depressions traveling north of the island, dry severely. The Central Plateau, in the rain shadow of the Massif du Nord, dries quickly and was the heart of the 2015-2016 crisis along with the Northwest.

### Artibonite rice, the special case

The Artibonite Valley produces approximately 80% of Haiti's rice supply (USDA FAS HA2025-0003). Rice is grown in two seasons — spring (May-October) and winter (December-April) — and depends on the Péligre hydroelectric dam and the Canal de l'Artibonite system rather than direct rainfall. This irrigation buffer matters enormously: during the 2015-16 event, even as rainfed maize fell ~39% and beans ~44%, USDA FAS yield estimates for Haitian rice held at 2.5 MT/ha rough basis — a flat trajectory that reflects irrigation insulation. The system is, however, vulnerable through two channels: first, prolonged El Niño drought eventually draws down Lac de Péligre reservoir storage and reduces hydroelectric and irrigation deliveries; second, infrastructure degradation (canal siltation, dyke failure) and recent armed-group occupation of parts of the Artibonite (per the Réseau National de Défense des Droits Humains/RNDDH 2025, at least 84 people were killed in 24 armed attacks across Artibonite localities in the first nine months of 2025) have eroded the irrigation system's capacity independent of climate. For 2026-27, the irrigation buffer is real but increasingly compromised by non-climate factors; rice production at the 1.8-1.9 MT/ha yield level forecast by USDA FAS for MY 2025/26 is the realistic baseline against which El Niño impact must be measured.

### Soil, watershed and antecedent conditions

Two non-climate amplifiers deserve emphasis. First, both countries enter the 2026-27 El Niño with degraded antecedent moisture conditions following the 2023-24 El Niño's prolonged drought and an insufficient 2024-25 La Niña recharge: SPEI-3 indices across the Dry Corridor and Haiti remained in the moderate-to-severe drought category through much of 2024-25. Second, deforestation rates in Haiti (forest cover <2%) and along the El Salvador-Honduras border imply that the same precipitation deficit now produces a larger runoff fraction and a smaller infiltration fraction than during the 1997-98 event, depressing shallow groundwater recharge for wells and small-scale irrigation. This is a slow-moving but cumulatively important biophysical shift: the 2026 event will encounter a more brittle hydrology than the 1997-98 event did.

### Why the canícula is the lynchpin

Anderson et al. (2023, *Geophysical Research Letters*, doi:10.1029/2023GL105391) demonstrated for the 2015-2019 Central American drought that "the severity of this drought was driven primarily by rainfall deficits in July-August" — i.e., the canícula window. This is biophysically consequential because primera maize is at V8-silking-grain-filling phenology during this period, the stage where maize yield-water-stress curves are steepest. The CGIAR Alliance report *El fenómeno de El Niño en Guatemala* (2024) found that across 1980-2021, over 60% of El Niño events produced moderate SPEI drought during the May-October agricultural season, with up to 20% precipitation reduction, 1°C temperature increase, and 7% potential-evapotranspiration increase — quantitative anchors that translate directly to El Salvador's eastern Dry Corridor.

### Misconceptions worth correcting

Three common misconceptions are worth flagging. First, the claim that El Niño "always" causes severe Caribbean drought is overstated; El Niño's effect on Caribbean rainfall is statistically robust but spatially heterogeneous, and Anderson et al. (2023) show that "the 2015–2019 drought was severe, but it falls within the range of natural climate variability." Indeed, UNDRR's 2023 forensic analysis notes that "since 1981, regional droughts have occurred roughly every seven years… El Niño is often assumed to be a factor, but in fact it triggered only half of the regional droughts in this period." Second, the assumption that hurricane suppression is purely protective for Haiti misses that TC and tropical-wave rainfall is a non-trivial fraction of August-November rain; the same dynamics that prevent a Matthew-like disaster also depress automne agricultural rainfall. Third, the framing that "El Niño caused" the 1997-98 Salvadoran agricultural crisis underweights Hurricane Mitch (October 1998), which struck during the rapid ENSO transition to La Niña and did the bulk of the agricultural damage; treating Mitch as an El Niño effect is technically incorrect.

### Honest caveat on the "acute upcoming El Niño" framing

The framing of an "acute" 2026-27 El Niño is plausible but not yet established. The May 2026 CPC discussion explicitly states that "no strength categorization [exceed] a 37% chance" and that "stronger El Niño events do not ensure strong impacts; they can only make certain impacts more likely." Strong characterisations in the popular press relying on the ECMWF dynamical model should be treated against the actual numbers: per the ECMWF Science Blog (Stockdale, 10 April 2026), C3S April ensemble members for September Niño-3.4 "range from about 1.7 ºC to 3.3 ºC," and Carbon Brief's 13-model 637-run survey produces a multi-model median of +2.2°C for September 2026. The spring predictability barrier remains operative until early June, after which forecast skill rises sharply. A realistic working hypothesis is that the 2026-27 event will be a moderate-to-strong El Niño with peak Niño-3.4 anomalies in the +1.7 to +2.5°C range and a non-trivial chance of crossing the +2.0°C "very strong" threshold; the 1997-98 super-El Niño analog (+2.4°C three-month peak) should not yet be assumed.

## Recommendations

**For research framing (academic).** Position the 2026-27 event analytically as a moderate-to-strong El Niño analog in the 2015-16 family, not the 1997-98 family. Use 2015-16 as the primary biophysical analog for Haiti (where 1997-98 effects on agriculture were modest) and as the primary analog for El Salvador's smallholder grain sector (where 2015-16 produced the cleanest documented yield collapses). Use 1997-98 as a secondary analog for maximum-case scenarios on Niño-3.4 SST anomalies, atmospheric circulation response, and global teleconnection strength, while recognising that the regional rainfall and crop impacts of 1997-98 differed considerably from 2015-16 in both countries.

**For monitoring and benchmark thresholds.** Track the CariCOF May-July 2026 Caribbean precipitation outlook against ASO: if Hispaniola lands in the "below-usual" tercile with high confidence by the July CariCOF update, Haiti printemps 2026 losses comparable to spring 2015 (>40% maize, >44% beans nationally) become the baseline scenario. Monitor CHIRPS-derived SPEI-3 for the eastern Dry Corridor departments of El Salvador (Morazán, San Miguel, La Unión, Usulután); if SPEI-3 falls below -1.5 for July-August 2026, primera-season losses similar to 2015 (60% maize, 80% beans in worst zones) should be expected and contingency seed reserves should be activated for the postrera. Monitor the Niño-3.4 trajectory through August-September 2026: an anomaly sustained above +1.5°C through ASO 2026 will, per the regional ENSO–rainfall regression coefficients in Anderson et al. (2023) and Hidalgo et al. (2019), increase Dry Corridor July-August rainfall deficits by approximately 20% versus the 1991-2020 baseline. Track Lac de Péligre water level and Artibonite Valley canal flow into Q4 2026: if Péligre levels drop to drought-of-record lows by November 2026, the irrigation buffer that protected rice in 2015-16 may not hold for 2026-27.

**For adaptation and operational priorities.** Pre-position drought-tolerant maize and bean varieties (e.g., ICTA-derived materials, EAP-Zamorano-bred drought-tolerant beans) in the Dry Corridor departments of El Salvador and in the Northwest and southern peninsula of Haiti, by April 2026. Activate FAO/WFP cash-transfer mechanisms before the start of postrera 2026 planting (August-September), not after harvest failure, to prevent the seed-exhaustion cascade observed in 2015-16. For Haiti, prioritise irrigation infrastructure rehabilitation in the Artibonite canal system to maintain the rice buffer through the event; this is a structural intervention with high climate co-benefit. For both countries, deploy soil-moisture sensor networks at department/municipality level to provide real-time crop-water-stress data complementary to satellite NDVI and SPEI; the 2015-16 event response was hampered by reliance on coarse satellite products.

**Decision-changing benchmarks.** If the June or July 2026 CPC discussion increases the probability of a "very strong" event to >50%, shift planning toward 1997-98-class impacts. If the August 2026 CariCOF outlook places Hispaniola in the "above-usual" precipitation tercile (unlikely but possible if the event develops with Modoki characteristics or if the CLLJ response is anomalously weak), shift Haiti contingency to flood-protection rather than drought-protection mode. If the 2026 primera/printemps planting in either country goes ahead with normal or above-normal rainfall through June, the 2026 postrera/automne becomes the primary risk window and analytical attention should shift accordingly.

## Caveats

ENSO–Central America rainfall relationships are statistically robust but explain only a fraction of total interannual variance; Anderson et al. (2023) find that the Atlantic Multidecadal Variability (AMV) and Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) are co-equal modulators of Central American rainfall, and attributing specific events purely to El Niño is methodologically risky. The 2023-24 Haitian agricultural decline is heavily confounded by gang violence, port closures, and input supply collapse, making it a noisy ENSO signal at best; USDA FAS explicitly attributes most of the 2023/24 rice decline to non-climate factors. The 1997-98 event's agricultural impact in Haiti was modest in FAO records, contrary to the assumption that "super" El Niños always devastate Haiti; the relevant analog for catastrophic Haiti impacts is 2015-16, not 1997-98. Quantitative Haiti-specific data on TC contribution to ASO rainfall remain a literature gap. The 2026-27 forecast remains subject to the spring predictability barrier through May-June 2026; quantitative SST forecasts beyond a Niño-3.4 range of roughly +1.5 to +3.3°C carry substantial ensemble spread, and the multi-model median of +2.2°C for September 2026 (Carbon Brief 13-model survey) is a more defensible central estimate than the popular "+3°C" headline figures. Finally, climate-change-elevated baseline temperatures mean that the same Niño-3.4 anomaly now produces more crop heat stress than during 1997-98 — a non-stationarity that limits the direct comparability of historical analogs and biases the 2026-27 expected impacts upward, particularly for night-temperature-sensitive crops like beans.
